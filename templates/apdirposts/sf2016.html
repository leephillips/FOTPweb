The General Theory of Relativity did not spring into being with a single, astonishing paper, as did the Special Theory of Relativity in 1905. Its birth was more chaotic, involving a handful of lectures and manuscripts, and more than one parent. But this harrowing labor finally resulted in the delivery of a Theory perfectly formed and trembling with potential, if not yet fully mature. The painful and joyous process occupied most of November, 2015, making this its 100^th^ birthday — an anniversary being celebrated by scientific organizations around the world. Albert Einstein in 1919, after the eclipse voyages that verified General Relativity. Public domain. The General Theory is our modern theory of gravity. Its fundamental equations have been unchanged since 1915, although we have learned a great deal more about their consequences in the past 100 years. This model of gravity, space, and time is regarded by many who know it well as perhaps the “most beautiful of all existing physical theories.” Perhaps the best approach to the General Theory of Relativity is by way of Isaac Newton and his theory of gravity. Newton’s gravity (in concert with his laws of motion) accurately predicted the motions of the heavenly bodies for over 200 years. It was the first great unification in physics, connecting our terrestrial experience with falling apples directly to the force that binds the solar system together. Newton’s work is the beginning of modern science. The best way to begin to understand relativity is to try to understand what Einstein found unacceptable in Newton’s model of the universe. Newton explained that gravity is a force between any two objects, proportional to the product of their masses, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them: a simple algebraic formula. This force was an instantaneous action at a distance, with no medium nor mechanism behind it.
